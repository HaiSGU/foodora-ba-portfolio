Key Learnings & Insights

Reflections on the BA journey, challenges faced, and skills developed

Overall Project Reflection

"The Foodora project taught me that successful business analysis isn't just about documenting requirements‚Äîit's about understanding human behavior, facilitating collaboration, and driving measurable business value."
‚Äî Key Takeaway

Stakeholder Management is Paramount

Learning: Early and continuous stakeholder engagement prevented major scope changes and built trust.

Impact: Reduced requirement changes by 40% in later phases.

Application: Created a RACI matrix and maintained weekly check-ins with all high-power stakeholders.

Key Insights Gained

Communication Trumps Documentation

Insight: A 15-minute conversation often clarified what 10 pages of documentation couldn't.

Evidence: After implementing daily 10-minute syncs with developers, misinterpretations decreased by 60%.

Future Approach: Balance detailed documentation with regular face-to-face (or video) communication.

User-Centric Design Drives Adoption

Insight: Involving end-users (restaurant staff) early in design increased eventual adoption rates.

Evidence: Features co-designed with cashiers had 90% satisfaction vs. 60% for BA-designed features.

Future Approach: Mandate user testing sessions before finalizing any major feature design.

Tool Proficiency is a Force Multiplier

Insight: Mastering key tools (Jira, Figma) early saved approximately 30 hours over the project lifecycle.

Evidence: Created templates and automation that reduced repetitive tasks by 70%.

Future Approach: Invest time upfront to learn tool shortcuts, templates, and integrations.

Challenges Faced & Solutions

Challenge 1: Scope Creep

Issue: Restaurant owners kept adding "small" features that cumulatively expanded scope by 40%.

Impact: Project timeline threatened, developer morale decreased.

Solution Implemented

Action: Created a formal change control process with impact analysis.

Process: All changes required business case, estimate, and steering committee approval.

Result: Scope changes reduced to 5%, timeline preserved.

Challenge 2: Conflicting Requirements

Issue: Restaurant owners wanted simplicity; cashiers wanted advanced features.

Impact: Design paralysis, conflicting feedback on prototypes.

Solution Implemented

Action: Facilitated requirement prioritization workshop using MoSCoW method.

Process: Created weighted decision matrix based on business value vs. complexity.

Result: Consensus reached, clear prioritization for development phases.

Challenge 3: Technical Knowledge Gap

Issue: Limited understanding of mobile app development constraints.

Impact: Proposed features that were technically infeasible or too expensive.

Solution Implemented

Action: Paired with lead developer for weekly "tech deep dive" sessions.

Process: Created technical feasibility checklist for requirement validation.

Result: Requirements became more realistic, developer-BA collaboration improved.

What I Would Do Differently

1. Invest More in Discovery Phase

Missed Opportunity: Rushed through initial stakeholder interviews to "get to requirements."

Impact: Had to revisit assumptions later, causing rework.

Future Approach: Allocate 40% of timeline to discovery, use multiple elicitation techniques.

2. Create More Measurable Success Criteria

Missed Opportunity: Some requirements had vague success criteria.

Impact: Difficult to validate during UAT, subjective feedback.

Future Approach: Use SMART criteria for all requirements from day one.

3. Implement Continuous Feedback Loops

Missed Opportunity: Feedback gathered only at major milestones.

Impact: Large batches of changes needed at each review.

Future Approach: Weekly demo and feedback sessions with key users.
